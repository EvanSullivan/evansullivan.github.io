Why I Rarely Build The Ideas I Think Of

October 29th, 2016

I frequently find myself stuck, not finding the motivation to make progress on current projects, and also not actually starting new ones. I believe the problem is due to how important I think a given project or idea is. If the idea isn’t super ideal, I don’t feel like it’s worth spending time on. If the idea does clear the bar of being really great, I end up worrying so much about getting my next step right, that I never do anything with it. My ideas tend to fall into one of two categories:

  1. A cool idea, but not quite perfect, so I’d better save my time to work on something better.
  2. A really great idea, but one that I haven’t made enough previous stuff to feel like I can nail the perfection this great idea deserves.
So my ideas all end up being either not perfect enough to be worth my time, or too incredible to risk making an imperfect attempt at. So of course I end up thinking about things quite a lot, but actually making nothing. I think the antidote requires two steps:
  1. Make a habit of getting the imperfect and incomplete versions of my ideas out of my head, and into the world. There’s even already a name for this kind of thing, a draft.
  2. Go back and look at my crappy draft, and instead of sulking over having made something crappy, notice that I can now look at this crappy version of my idea in the world and see how to make it a little better.
Repeat this.
